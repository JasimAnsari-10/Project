Bhagat Singh

Shaheed-e-azam

1907-1931


Born: 28 September 1907, Banga, Pakistan.
Died: 23 March 1931 (age 23 years), Lahore Central Jail, Lahore, Pakistan.
Education: DAV College Managing Committee (1916–1917), National College, Lahore.
Siblings: Bibi Parkash Kaur, Kultar Singh, Bibi Amar Kaur.

Bhagat Singh (1907–1931) was one of the most influential revolutionaries in the Indian independence movement against British colonial rule. Born in Punjab, India, Bhagat Singh was deeply inspired by the ideals of socialism and revolution from a young age. He was a key figure in the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA), a group of young revolutionaries who believed in using radical measures to fight British imperialism.


Here are some key points about Bhagat Singh:


Early Life:

  • Birthplace: Bhagat Singh was born on September 28, 1907, in Banga, Punjab (now in Pakistan). His family was politically active, which shaped his early understanding of the struggle for India's independence.

  • Inspiration: He was inspired by prominent freedom fighters like Lala Lajpat Rai and events like the Jallianwala Bagh massacre, which fueled his desire to end British rule.


Revolutionary Activities:

  • HSRA Membership: Bhagat Singh joined the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) to achieve freedom through revolutionary means. He believed in the power of armed resistance and socialism.

  • Lahore Conspiracy Case: Bhagat Singh, along with his comrades, was involved in planning a series of attacks on British officials. He is perhaps best known for his involvement in the killing of John Saunders, a British police officer, in 1928. This act was a response to the police lathi charge that resulted in the death of nationalist leader Lala Lajpat Rai.

  • Assembly Bombing: On April 8, 1929, Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt threw non-lethal bombs in the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi. The intent was not to harm anyone but to make a powerful statement against British rule. After throwing the bombs, they shouted slogans like "Inquilab Zindabad" (Long live the revolution) and willingly surrendered to the police.


Imprisonment and Execution:

  • Bhagat Singh's trial, along with his associates Rajguru and Sukhdev, became a national issue. During his imprisonment, he used the court as a platform to express his political ideology, advocating for the complete independence of India.

  • Execution: On March 23, 1931, Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev were hanged in Lahore (now in Pakistan) for their involvement in the Saunders murder. This date is now commemorated as Shaheed Diwas (Martyrs' Day) in India.


Legacy

Bhagat Singh became a symbol of courage and resistance in the fight for independence. His writings, speeches, and fearless attitude inspired generations of Indians to challenge British rule. He is often remembered for his revolutionary zeal and his commitment to justice, equality, and freedom.

Bhagat Singh's famous quote, "It is easy to kill individuals but you cannot kill ideas", continues to inspire activists and movements around the world.
